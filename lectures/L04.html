s4.1 — SIFT Overview & Invariances

What is SIFT?

Why it matters: SIFT is the canonical classical-CV feature pipeline. Almost every "match these two photos" task in classical CV (panorama stitching, object recognition, 3-D reconstruction, tracking) sits on top of it.

SIFT (Scale-Invariant Feature Transform) is a feature detection & description algorithm introduced by David G. Lowe in 1999 and refined in 2004. It detects distinctive "keypoints" in an image and produces a vector descriptor for each so that the same physical point on an object can be re-identified in a different photo of the same scene.

Two-step slogan to memorise: [1] Detect keypoints → [2] Describe them.

What is a "keypoint" / "interesting point"?

Why it matters: every subsequent step in SIFT is built to deliver one good keypoint type — a blob — rather than corners or pure edges.

A keypoint is just a point in the image that we can find again later. Corners and points on edges are okay points but not very distinctive (corners look like every other corner). What we really want is a blob: a small region with some local appearance — a bright spot, dark spot, or textured patch — that is unique enough to match.

Interpret this image — why a blob beats a corner A corner in a grid of corners is non-unique: hundreds of corners look identical. A blob has texture and intensity contrast in all directions inside its support region, so its 128-D descriptor (s4.4) is much more distinctive — far easier to match across two photos.

SIFT properties — the six invariances

Why it matters: exam loves "list the four/six invariances and explain how SIFT achieves each one". You can lose easy marks here.

  1. Scale invariance — detects the same feature at different image scales (sizes). Achieved by searching for extrema in scale-space (s4.2).
  2. Rotation invariance — features are detected independently of image orientation. Achieved by assigning a dominant orientation to each keypoint and rotating the descriptor window to that orientation (s4.4).
  3. Affine-transform invariance (partial) — handles scaling + rotation simultaneously, plus small shears.
  4. 3-D viewpoint invariance — features stay stable under small changes in 3-D viewpoint, because they describe local image patches that vary slowly with viewpoint.
  5. Illumination invariance — uniform changes in lighting don't break matching, because the descriptor is built from gradients (differences) which are unaffected by adding a constant brightness, then normalised.
  6. Occlusion invariance — partial occlusion is fine, because each keypoint is local and does not depend on the global image.

One-liner per invariance — memorise

  • Scale → scale-space (Gaussian pyramid + DoG).
  • Rotation → dominant orientation from 36-bin gradient histogram.
  • Affine → both above simultaneously.
  • Viewpoint → local patches change slowly with small viewpoint moves.
  • Illumination → gradients (immune to additive lighting) + descriptor normalisation.
  • Occlusion → each keypoint is local; lose some, keep the rest.

Common exam traps

  • "SIFT is fully affine invariant." — False. Partial affine only (scale + rotation, small shears). Fully affine is ASIFT / Affine-SIFT.
  • "SIFT works on rotated images because the keypoint detector is rotation-symmetric." — partly: the DoG detector is roughly isotropic, but the real rotation invariance comes from rotating the descriptor to the dominant orientation.
  • "Illumination invariance means SIFT survives any lighting change." — only uniform additive / multiplicative changes; harsh shadows, specular highlights and saturation can still kill matches.
Q. Which two invariances combined explain SIFT's affine robustness?
AnswerScale invariance and rotation invariance, handled at the same time.

Applications of SIFT

Why it matters: exam regularly asks "name 3 applications of SIFT".

  • Image matching — find corresponding points between two photos.
  • Image alignment & stitching — used for panorama creation.
  • Object / scene recognition — recognise by unique feature points.
  • 3-D structure recovery — multi-view geometry.
  • Stereo correspondence — match left/right image points.
  • Motion tracking — keep features across video frames.

The 5-step SIFT pipeline at a glance

  1. Generate octaves of the input image (different image sizes).
  2. Keypoint detection at different scales (DoG extrema).
  3. Refinement of detected keypoints (sub-pixel localisation + low-contrast / edge rejection).
  4. Keypoint attributes: (position, scale, orientation).
  5. Build the descriptor (128-D vector) for each keypoint.

s4.2 — Scale space + Difference of Gaussians (DoG)

Motivation — why we need a scale space

Why it matters: an object can appear at any size depending on its distance from the camera. A feature detector that works at one scale will miss the same feature at another. We need to "deal with scale".

Quick review — edges, derivatives, and why we smooth first

Why it matters: SIFT's blob detector is built on derivatives of a smoothed image. Without smoothing, derivatives just amplify noise.

1-D derivatives of an image function:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\qquad \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}$$

1st-derivative peaks indicate an edge; 2nd-derivative zero-crossings indicate an edge.

Gaussian blur and the scale-space image $L(x,y,\sigma)$

Why it matters: the Gaussian is the unique kernel that builds a proper scale-space (no new structure is created as $\sigma$ grows). Everything in SIFT lives in this scale-space.

2-D Gaussian kernel:

$$G(x,y,\sigma)=\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^{2}}\,\exp\!\Big(-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\sigma^{2}}\Big)$$

Scale-space image (Gaussian-blurred version of the input $I$):

$$L(x,y,\sigma)=G(x,y,\sigma)\,*\,I(x,y)$$

Named symbols: $\sigma$ = standard deviation of the Gaussian (= "scale"); $*$ = 2-D convolution; $L$ = blurred image at scale $\sigma$.

Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) and its normalised version (NLoG)

Why it matters: LoG is the textbook "blob detector". SIFT uses an approximation of it for speed, but you must know what is being approximated.

Laplacian of Gaussian (2-D, "Mexican hat"):

$$\text{LoG}(x,y,\sigma)=-\frac{1}{\pi\sigma^{4}}\Big[1-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\sigma^{2}}\Big]\exp\!\Big(-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\sigma^{2}}\Big)$$

Problem: the LoG response shrinks with $\sigma$, so blobs of different sizes give incomparable responses.

Fix: multiply by $\sigma^{2}$ to get the normalised LoG (NLoG):

$$\text{NLoG}=\sigma^{2}\,\nabla^{2}G\,*\,I$$

Difference of Gaussians (DoG) — the SIFT speed trick

Why it matters: NLoG is expensive to compute. SIFT approximates it by simply subtracting two Gaussian-blurred images. This is the single most important shortcut in the algorithm.

Definition:

$$D(x,y,\sigma)=L(x,y,k\sigma)-L(x,y,\sigma)$$

where $k$ is a scale-multiplier between two consecutive blurred images. Named symbols: $D$ = DoG image; $L(\cdot,k\sigma)$ = image blurred at the higher scale; $L(\cdot,\sigma)$ = image blurred at the lower scale.

Why does DoG approximate $\sigma^{2}\nabla^{2}G$? From the heat equation / scale-space derivative,

$$\frac{\partial G}{\partial \sigma}=\sigma\,\nabla^{2}G\quad\Rightarrow\quad G(x,y,k\sigma)-G(x,y,\sigma)\;\approx\;(k-1)\,\sigma^{2}\,\nabla^{2}G$$

so

$$\boxed{\;\text{DoG}\;\approx\;(k-1)\,\sigma^{2}\nabla^{2}G\;=\;(k-1)\,\text{NLoG}\;}$$

Worked example — why "5 blurred → 4 DoGs → 2 candidate scales" per octave Slide 31 / 33 / 34 in the deck show the standard SIFT setting with $s=3$ scales per octave, so $k=2^{1/s}=2^{1/3}\approx 1.26$. To search for an extremum at scales $\sigma, k\sigma, k^{2}\sigma$ we need DoG images above and below each of those scales → we need 5 blurred images per octave (so 4 consecutive differences = 4 DoGs), and the extrema search runs on the middle 2 candidate DoG scales (the topmost and bottommost DoG have no neighbour above/below). After one octave, downsample the image by 2 and repeat.

Stacking it all together: octaves & scales

Why it matters: this is the "scale space" — a pyramid of octaves, each with multiple Gaussian blurs, and from each octave a stack of DoG images.

Worked numeric example — octave count for a 512 × 512 image

Each octave halves the image size. From 512 → 256 → 128 → 64 → 32 → 16 → 8 → 4. A typical SIFT pipeline stops before things get tiny (say, when the side is ≤ 32 pixels), giving 5 octaves: 512, 256, 128, 64, 32. Per octave, $s=3$ scales → 5 Gaussian blurs → 4 DoGs → 2 DoGs available for extrema search.

Common exam traps

  • "SIFT uses LoG directly." — False. It uses DoG as a cheap approximation of normalised LoG.
  • Forgetting the $\sigma^{2}$ normalisation factor — without it, responses are not comparable across scales.
  • Confusing "octave" (image size) and "scale" (different $\sigma$ within one octave). Octaves are size-halving steps; scales are $\sigma$ steps inside an octave.
  • "DoG = LoG exactly." — No, DoG ≈ $(k-1)\,\sigma^{2}\nabla^{2}G$.
Q. Why do we multiply LoG by $\sigma^{2}$?
AnswerSo responses at different scales are comparable; otherwise the LoG response shrinks as $\sigma$ grows and small blobs always "win".
Q. If a SIFT implementation uses $s=3$ scales per octave, what is the scale multiplier $k$?
Answer$k=2^{1/s}=2^{1/3}\approx 1.2599$.

s4.3 — Keypoint Localisation

[1] Detect initial extrema in 3-D scale-space (26 neighbours)

Why it matters: this is the exact criterion that turns a DoG stack into a list of candidate keypoints. It is also the #1 numeric exam question on this topic.

For each DoG image, compare every pixel against its 26 neighbours in the 3-D $(x,y,\sigma)$ scale-space:

  • 8 neighbours in the same DoG scale (the 3×3 window around it minus the centre).
  • 9 neighbours in the DoG scale immediately above (a 3×3 patch).
  • 9 neighbours in the DoG scale immediately below (a 3×3 patch).

Total: $8+9+9=26$ comparisons. If the pixel is greater than all 26 (local maximum) or smaller than all 26 (local minimum), it becomes a candidate keypoint.

Interpret this image — three DoG levels (L−1, L, L+1) The pixel marked "×" lies in the target DoG level $L$. We compare it to its 8 neighbours in $L$, then to the 9-pixel 3×3 patch at $L-1$ (above in scale), then to the 9-pixel 3×3 patch at $L+1$ (below in scale). Both upper and lower patches are full 3×3 (they include the pixel directly above/below "×"), so they contribute 9 neighbours each, not 8.
Q. How many neighbours does each pixel compare with in the SIFT extremum test, and why?
Answer26 = 8 in the same DoG scale + 9 in the scale above + 9 in the scale below. The scale-space is 3-D so the 3×3×3 cube around the pixel has $27-1=26$ neighbours.

[2] Sub-pixel / sub-scale refinement via Taylor series

Why it matters: the extremum found in step 1 is only at integer pixel + integer scale; the real extremum lives between samples. The Taylor refinement nudges it to its true location.

Expand the DoG function $D$ around the candidate point as a 2-nd-order Taylor series:

$$D(\mathbf{x})\;=\;D\;+\;\frac{\partial D}{\partial \mathbf{x}}^{\!\top}\mathbf{x}\;+\;\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{x}^{\!\top}\frac{\partial^{2}D}{\partial \mathbf{x}^{2}}\mathbf{x}$$

Set the gradient to zero to find the offset $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ of the true extremum from the sampled point:

$$\hat{\mathbf{x}}\;=\;-\Big(\frac{\partial^{2}D}{\partial\mathbf{x}^{2}}\Big)^{\!-1}\frac{\partial D}{\partial\mathbf{x}}$$

Named symbols: $\mathbf{x}=(x,y,\sigma)$ = offset; $\partial D/\partial\mathbf{x}$ = 3-vector gradient of DoG; $\partial^{2}D/\partial\mathbf{x}^{2}$ = $3\!\times\!3$ Hessian; $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ = sub-pixel/sub-scale offset.

[3] Filtering unstable keypoints — low contrast + edge response

Why it matters: even after refinement, lots of candidates sit on flat noise or along strong edges. Both kinds are unreliable. SIFT throws them away with two thresholds.

(3-1) Low-contrast rejection

Image noise produces spurious DoG extrema with small magnitude. Discard a candidate if

$$|D(\hat{\mathbf{x}})|\;<\;C$$

where $C$ is a contrast threshold (Lowe's paper uses 0.03 on $[0,1]$-normalised images). Result: more noise-immune keypoints.

(3-2) Edge-response rejection (Hessian principal-curvature ratio)

Candidates lying on an edge have high DoG response but slide along the edge — they are not well-localised. The 2-D Hessian of DoG at the keypoint tells us the local curvatures:

$$H=\begin{bmatrix}D_{xx} & D_{xy}\\ D_{xy} & D_{yy}\end{bmatrix}$$

Its eigenvalues $\alpha,\beta$ are the two principal curvatures. Let $r = \alpha/\beta$ be their ratio. For a true blob both eigenvalues are similar (small $r$). For an edge, one eigenvalue is much larger than the other (large $r$).

We don't need the eigenvalues themselves — we can read off the ratio from the trace and determinant:

$$\text{tr}\,H=\alpha+\beta,\qquad \det H=\alpha\beta$$

$$\frac{(\text{tr}\,H)^{2}}{\det H}=\frac{(r+1)^{2}}{r}$$

Keep a candidate iff

$$\boxed{\;\frac{(\text{tr}\,H)^{2}}{\det H}\;<\;\frac{(r+1)^{2}}{r}\;}$$

Lowe uses $r=10$, giving a threshold of $121/10=12.1$. Symbols: $\alpha,\beta$ = Hessian eigenvalues (principal curvatures); $r$ = max curvature / min curvature ratio.

Worked example — does a candidate with $r_{measured}=5$ survive at $r=10$? $(r+1)^{2}/r$ at $r=10$ is $121/10=12.1$. For a candidate whose measured ratio is 5, the LHS is $(5+1)^{2}/5=36/5=7.2$. Since $7.2<12.1$, the candidate passes the edge-response filter. A candidate with measured ratio 20 would give $(21)^{2}/20=22.05>12.1$ and would be rejected as edge-like.

Common exam traps

  • Confusing $r$ in the edge filter (ratio of Hessian eigenvalues) with the radius around a keypoint.
  • Forgetting the inequality direction — we keep points with $(\text{tr})^{2}/\det < (r+1)^{2}/r$, we reject those above.
  • "All edge points are rejected." — only those above the threshold. A weak edge whose curvature ratio < 10 may still pass.
Q. Why does a high $r$ (ratio of curvatures) signal an edge rather than a blob?
AnswerA blob has roughly equal curvature in every direction ($\alpha\approx\beta$, $r\approx 1$). An edge curves sharply across the edge but is flat along it, so one principal curvature is much larger than the other → $r$ is large.
Q. Lowe's contrast threshold $C=0.03$ is applied to the DoG value at the refined location. True or false?
AnswerTrue — the refinement step gives the more accurate $|D(\hat{\mathbf{x}})|$, which is then compared with $C$.

s4.4 — Orientation assignment + 128-D descriptor

[1] Assign a dominant orientation to each keypoint (36-bin histogram)

Why it matters: this is what makes SIFT rotation-invariant. The descriptor window will later be rotated to this canonical direction so that the resulting 128-D vector looks the same regardless of in-plane rotation.

For each surviving keypoint at scale $\sigma$:

  1. Define a square / circular neighbourhood around the keypoint of radius $3\sigma$ (the "3$\sigma$ region" — covers ~99 % of the Gaussian).
  2. At every pixel $(x,y)$ in that neighbourhood compute the gradient magnitude $m$ and orientation $\theta$ from the scale-space image $L$:

$$m(x,y)=\sqrt{\big(L(x+1,y)-L(x-1,y)\big)^{2}+\big(L(x,y+1)-L(x,y-1)\big)^{2}}$$

$$\theta(x,y)=\tan^{-1}\!\frac{L(x,y+1)-L(x,y-1)}{L(x+1,y)-L(x-1,y)}$$

  1. Build a 36-bin orientation histogram (one bin per 10°) of these gradient orientations, where each vote is weighted by:
    • the gradient magnitude $m$, and
    • a Gaussian window centred at the keypoint (so pixels far away count less than pixels right at the keypoint).
  2. Find the peak bin → this is the dominant orientation of the keypoint. Any other bin within 80 % of the peak height creates a second keypoint at the same $(x,y,\sigma)$ with that alternative orientation.
Why radius $3\sigma$? — see slide 46/47 sketch Because ~99.7 % of a Gaussian's mass lies inside $\pm 3\sigma$. A bigger radius adds almost no information but more computation; a smaller radius cuts off real signal.

[2] Build the 128-D descriptor (4 × 4 grid × 8 bins)

Why it matters: this 128-number vector is the SIFT feature — everything else (matching, recognition, stitching) reads only these numbers.

  1. Take a 16 × 16 pixel window centred at the keypoint.
  2. Rotate the window by the keypoint's dominant orientation → makes the descriptor rotation-invariant.
  3. Divide the rotated 16 × 16 window into a 4 × 4 grid of 4 × 4 cells (16 cells, each cell has 16 pixels).
  4. In each 4 × 4 cell, compute an 8-bin gradient-orientation histogram (one bin per 45°), again Gaussian-weighted.
  5. Concatenate the 16 histograms × 8 bins each → $4\times 4\times 8=128$-dimensional vector.
  6. Normalise the vector to unit length to discount linear contrast changes; clip every component to ≤ 0.2 and re-normalise to discount non-linear illumination (saturation, etc.).

Why does this give illumination invariance?

  • Adding a constant to every pixel doesn't change gradients, so the histogram is unchanged.
  • Multiplying every pixel by a constant scales the gradient magnitudes uniformly — the L2 normalisation cancels it.
  • Clipping at 0.2 then re-normalising handles non-linear changes (saturation, harsh shadows).
Worked numeric example — confirm 128 $4\;\text{rows}\;\times\;4\;\text{cols}\;\times\;8\;\text{orientation bins} = 128$. Each cell is itself $4\times4=16$ pixels, so the descriptor summarises $16\times16=256$ pixels into 128 numbers — roughly a 2:1 compression with a much more invariant representation.

Common exam traps

  • "36-bin histogram for descriptors." — No. 36 bins are for the orientation-assignment step. The descriptor uses 8 bins per cell.
  • "Window is 4 × 4 pixels." — No: 16 × 16 pixels, divided into 4 × 4 cells.
  • Descriptor dimension is 128, not 16 or 32.
  • Forgetting the normalisation → illumination invariance disappears.
Q (numeric). How many numbers are in a SIFT descriptor vector? Show the arithmetic.
Answer$4\times 4\times 8=128$.

Matching two SIFT descriptors — the nearest-neighbour-ratio test

Why it matters: a SIFT keypoint is only useful if we can decide whether two descriptors come from the same physical point. Lowe's ratio test is the standard rule.

Given a descriptor $\mathbf{d}$ in image A, find its two closest descriptors $\mathbf{d}_{1},\mathbf{d}_{2}$ in image B (by Euclidean / L2 distance). Compute:

$$\rho=\frac{\|\mathbf{d}-\mathbf{d}_{1}\|}{\|\mathbf{d}-\mathbf{d}_{2}\|}$$

Accept the match $(\mathbf{d}\leftrightarrow\mathbf{d}_{1})$ iff $\rho<0.8$. If the nearest neighbour is not clearly better than the second-nearest, the match is ambiguous and is dropped.

Worked example — does a match pass the 0.8 ratio test? Suppose for a keypoint in image A the nearest neighbour in image B is at L2 distance 120, and the second-nearest at 200. Ratio $\rho = 120/200 = 0.6 < 0.8$ → accept. If the distances had been 180 and 200, $\rho=0.9>0.8$ → reject.

Practice quiz — Lecture 4

1 (Numeric). Compute the dimensionality of a SIFT descriptor.
Answer$4 \times 4 \times 8 = 128$ (4×4 spatial grid, 8 orientation bins per cell).
2 (MCQ). In SIFT's scale-space extremum step, each pixel is compared to how many neighbours?
a) 8   b) 9   c) 26   d) 27
Answerc) 26 = 8 (same DoG scale) + 9 (above) + 9 (below).
3 (T/F). DoG exactly equals the Laplacian of Gaussian.
AnswerFalse. DoG ≈ $(k-1)\,\sigma^{2}\nabla^{2}G$ — an approximation of the normalised LoG.
4 (Short). State Lowe's edge-response rejection inequality (using trace and determinant of the Hessian).
AnswerKeep iff $(\text{tr}\,H)^{2}/\det H < (r+1)^{2}/r$ with $r$ typically 10 ⇒ threshold $12.1$.
5 (Short). For the orientation-assignment histogram, how many bins are used, what is the bin width, and how are votes weighted?
Answer36 bins, 10° per bin; each vote is weighted by the gradient magnitude and a Gaussian centred at the keypoint.
6 (Scenario). You photograph the same book cover twice: once in shadow, once under a lamp. Why does SIFT still match the two?
AnswerDescriptors are built from gradients (immune to additive lighting changes) and then L2-normalised (cancels multiplicative changes); clipping at 0.2 and re-normalising handles saturation/non-linear lighting.
7 (MCQ). The Lowe nearest-neighbour-ratio test accepts a match if the ratio of the nearest / 2nd-nearest descriptor distance is below:
a) 0.2   b) 0.5   c) 0.8   d) 1.0
Answerc) 0.8.
8 (Numeric). With $s=3$ scales per octave, how many Gaussian-blurred images and how many DoG images are produced per octave, and on how many DoG scales are extrema actually searched?
Answer5 Gaussian blurs → 4 DoGs → extrema search on the middle 2 DoG scales (top and bottom DoG can't be tested against an above/below scale).
9 (Short). List the four "main" invariances of SIFT and, in one phrase, how each is achieved.
AnswerScale — scale-space (Gaussian pyramid + DoG). Rotation — dominant orientation via 36-bin gradient histogram. Illumination — gradient-based + normalised descriptor. Occlusion — keypoints are local, so partial loss is fine. (Also: partial-affine and 3-D viewpoint.)
10 (Numeric — edge-response). A candidate keypoint has Hessian trace $\text{tr}\,H=12$ and determinant $\det H=20$. With Lowe's threshold $r=10$, is it kept or rejected?
Answer$(\text{tr}\,H)^{2}/\det H = 144/20 = 7.2$. Threshold $(r+1)^{2}/r = 121/10 = 12.1$. Since $7.2 < 12.1$, the candidate is kept.
