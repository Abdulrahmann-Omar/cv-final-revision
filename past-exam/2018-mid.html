📝 Question 2 — SIFT, RANSAC, Mean-shift

Question 2·A — SIFT: scale-space, DoG vs LoG, scale & rotation invariance

i) What is "scale-space"?

"Scale-space" is the same image at many levels of blur. Think of a stack of copies of the picture, each one slightly more blurred than the one below it — you can see big structures at the top and fine detail at the bottom.
Formally: convolve the image with Gaussians of increasing σ. The set of blurred images {L(x, y, σ)} across all σ is the scale-space. It lets the detector look for the same feature whether the camera is close (large detail) or far (small detail).

ii) What is DoG used for? Why prefer it to LoG?

DoG = subtract two adjacent blurred images. The result is a near-perfect cheap copy of the Laplacian-of-Gaussian at that scale — so it's used to find keypoints (blob-like extrema) across scale.
Use: in SIFT, after building the Gaussian pyramid, you compute D(x, y, σ) = L(x, y, kσ) − L(x, y, σ) at each level. A pixel that is a local extremum across its 8 neighbours in the current DoG image and the 9 neighbours in the DoG image above and below ⇒ keypoint candidate.
DoG advantages over LoG:
  • Much cheaper — you already had to build the Gaussian pyramid, so DoG is "free" (one subtraction per level) whereas LoG needs an extra second-derivative filter at every scale.
  • Numerically stable — DoG ≈ (k − 1) σ² ∇²G, so it directly gives you the scale-normalised Laplacian SIFT actually wants.

iii) How does SIFT achieve scale and rotation invariance?

Scale invariance comes from where you find the keypoint; rotation invariance comes from how you describe it.
  1. Scale invariance: keypoints are detected as extrema in the DoG scale-space. The σ at which the extremum is found is stored with the keypoint, and all later measurements (descriptor window size, gradient sampling) are scaled by that σ. So the same physical feature is detected at the same relative scale no matter how big or small it appears in the image.
  2. Rotation invariance: at each keypoint, build a histogram of local gradient orientations (weighted by gradient magnitude and a Gaussian centred on the keypoint). The peak of the histogram is the keypoint's dominant orientation. The 16×16 descriptor patch is then rotated so that this dominant direction points "up" before sampling. The same patch, rotated by any angle, will always be aligned the same way before the descriptor is computed.
In one line: scale invariance = detect at the right σ in DoG scale-space; rotation invariance = rotate the patch to its dominant gradient direction before describing it.

Question 2·B — RANSAC: main stages, and the model fitted for panorama stitching

Main stages of RANSAC (general case)

RANSAC = "guess from a tiny random sample, then count how many real points agree". You repeat that guess-and-count many times and keep the best guess. It works because a few clean correspondences are enough to define the model, and outliers will never agree on the same model by accident.
  1. Pick a minimal random sample. Choose the smallest number of points that uniquely defines your model (e.g. 2 points for a line, 4 point-pairs for a homography, 3 for an affine transform).
  2. Fit a candidate model to that tiny sample (closed-form — no optimisation needed, the sample is minimal).
  3. Score the model on all data: count how many of the remaining points lie within an error threshold of it. Those agreeing points are the inliers.
  4. Keep the best. If the inlier count beats the current best, save this model and its inliers.
  5. Repeat for N iterations (N is chosen so that, with high probability, at least one sample was outlier-free).
  6. Refit on all inliers of the best model using least-squares — that's the final returned model.

Which model does RANSAC fit when stitching a panorama?

When you stitch two photos taken by rotating the camera about its centre, every point in one image is a linear warp of its counterpart in the other — translation, rotation, scaling, possibly a small shear. That's exactly what an affine (or, more strictly, a 2D homography) transform describes.
Answer: RANSAC is used to fit an affine transformation (in the more general case, a 2D homography / projective transform) between the two images — 6 parameters for affine, 8 for homography. The inliers are the matched SIFT keypoints that survive the geometric check; the outliers are mismatches.

Question 2·C — Mean-shift vs K-means for image segmentation

Advantage of Mean-shift over K-means

The killer difference: K-means makes you guess K, Mean-shift figures it out from the data. Everything else flows from that.
K-meansMean-shift
Number of clustersYou must pick K up front.Discovered automatically — equals the number of density modes found.
Cluster shapeAssumes round (isotropic) blobs — fails on elongated or curved regions.Any shape — clusters follow the actual density modes in feature space.
InitialisationSensitive — different random seeds give different segmentations.Deterministic — every pixel converges to its own density peak.
OutliersDistort the cluster means.Handled gracefully — they sit on low-density tails and don't drag a mode.
Bottom line: for image segmentation, Mean-shift is better because you don't have to know how many regions are in the image, and the regions can be any shape. K-means forces a number-of-clusters guess and squashes everything into round blobs in colour space.

The price: Mean-shift is slower (every pixel does its own gradient ascent on the kernel-density estimate) and the bandwidth h has to be chosen — but that one parameter controls scale, not count.
