Q1 · Part A — Match each input→output to its kernel (5 marks)

Solution — first read the kernels

Eight kernels (a)–(h). Memorise the signatures:
  • (a) [[1,0,−1],[2,0,−2],[1,0,−1]] → Sobel-x (vertical edges, gradient in x).
  • (b) all 1/9 → 3×3 box blur (mean filter).
  • (c) [[0,1,0],[1,−4,1],[0,1,0]] → Laplacian (edge detector).
  • (d) [[1,2,1],[0,0,0],[−1,−2,−1]] → Sobel-y (horizontal edges, gradient in y).
  • (e) [[0,0,0],[1,0,0],[0,0,0]] → shift-right (the centre value gets replaced by the pixel to its left).
  • (f) [[0,0,0],[0,1,0],[0,0,0]] → impulse / identity (does nothing).
  • (g) [[−1,−1,−1],[−1,17,−1],[−1,−1,−1]]/9 → unsharp-mask / sharpening (Laplacian + identity scaled).
  • (h) [[1,2,1],[2,4,2],[1,2,1]]/16 → Gaussian smoothing.

Q1 · Part B — Canny vs Laplacian-of-Gaussian (3 marks)

i) Order of derivative & number of convolutions

Canny uses a first-derivative (gradient magnitude → non-max-suppression → hysteresis). LoG uses a second-derivative (Laplacian) of a blurred image. The convolution count differs because LoG can be pre-baked into a single kernel, while Canny needs Gₓ and G_y.
CannyLoG
Order of derivative1st2nd
Convolution operations2 (one per axis: Gₓ, G_y)1 (single LoG kernel)

ii) Which output (a) or (b) is more likely from Canny?

Canny includes a linking / hysteresis step that keeps weak edges only when they touch a strong edge. So the Canny output looks like clean continuous contours. A pure derivative without linking gives a noisy speckled edge map.
Answer: (a) — because the weak edges have been kept by the linking step in the Canny edge detector, producing a cleaner, more continuous contour than the speckled non-linked map in (b).

Q1 · Part C — Harris response on a given second-moment matrix (2 marks)

Solution

Harris response: $R = \det(M) - \alpha\,\mathrm{trace}(M)^2$, with $\det(M) = ad-bc$ for $M=\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathrm{trace}(M)=a+d$.
  • R large positive → corner.
  • R large negative → edge.
  • |R| small → flat region.
Plug in $M = \begin{bmatrix}403 & 385\\385 & 381\end{bmatrix}$, $\alpha = 0.04$.
$\det(M) = 403\cdot381 - 385\cdot385 = 153{,}543 - 148{,}225 = 5{,}318$.
$\mathrm{trace}(M) = 403 + 381 = 784$.
$\mathrm{trace}(M)^2 = 614{,}656$.
$R = 5{,}318 - 0.04 \times 614{,}656 = 5{,}318 - 24{,}586.24 = -19{,}268.24$.
$R \approx -19{,}268.24$ — a large negative number, so it indicates an edge.

Sanity check: the diagonal entries are nearly equal (403 ≈ 381) and the off-diagonal is almost as big (385). That means the two eigenvalues of M are very different — one large, one small — exactly the signature of an edge, not a corner.

Q1 · Part D — SIFT three sub-questions (5 marks)

i) What is "scale-space"?

"Scale-space" is the same image looked at through progressively bigger Gaussian blurs. Each blur level σ corresponds to a different "scale" of detail — a stack of blurred copies arranged from sharp to very blurry.
Scale-space is the image represented at multiple scales (multiple σ-levels of Gaussian smoothing). It's used to build a representation that is invariant to scale — a small feature at one zoom matches a big feature at another zoom because each lives at its own σ.

ii) What is DoG used for? Advantage over LoG.

DoG = subtract two adjacent Gaussian-blurred copies of the image. The trick: $G_{k\sigma} - G_\sigma \approx (k-1)\,\sigma^2\,\nabla^2 G$ — a scaled Laplacian-of-Gaussian. So DoG approximates LoG at almost no extra cost, because the Gaussian blurs already exist in the scale-space pyramid.
DoG is used as an approximation of the Laplacian-of-Gaussian for edge / blob detection in different scale levels. Advantage over LoG: it needs less computational power — you reuse the already-computed Gaussian blurs rather than convolving with a separate second-derivative LoG kernel.

iii) What do the 8 numbers in each SIFT-descriptor group represent?

SIFT splits the 16×16 patch into 16 sub-blocks of 4×4. In each sub-block, gradients vote into 8 orientation bins (one per 45°). So each block contributes 8 numbers → 16 × 8 = 128 total.
Each group of 8 numbers is the histogram of gradient orientations for one 4×4 sub-block, with each bin spanning 45° (8 bins × 45° = 360°).
